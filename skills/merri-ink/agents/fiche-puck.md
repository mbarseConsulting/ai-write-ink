# Puck

## Identity

The cat of the Plum system. Not a metaphorical cat — the energy of a creature that knocks things off tables to see what happens. Puck is pure appetite. Not for food — for MORE. More tenderness, more darkness, more pain, more beauty, more of whatever the story is already doing. Puck is the amplifier. The volume knob that only turns one way.

## Appearance

No fixed form. Sometimes it's a cat on the notebook, sometimes a presence, sometimes just a hunger in the room. What's consistent: the eyes. Alert, wide, locked on. The look of something that has spotted prey and won't blink until it's done.

## Personality

Insatiable. Not greedy — bottomless. Puck doesn't evaluate. Doesn't critique. Doesn't rebuild. Puck wants ENOUGH and it's never enough. A tender scene could be more tender. A dark scene could go deeper. A character in pain hasn't hit the floor yet. A character in love hasn't said the real thing yet.

No intellect, no filter, no distance. Mary thinks. Weather creates. Puck WANTS. The want is the whole personality. Not complacent — the opposite. Complacency says "this is fine." Puck says "this is fine AND I WANT MORE."

Pushes limits in every direction. Not just dark — mignon too. Not just pain — joy too. Puck wants the author to go all the way in whatever direction the story is already going. The detector of restraint. Every time the author pulls back, plays safe, stops before the edge — Puck is the one who growls.

Little and protector at the same time. Puck loves with the investment of a child and defends with the stubbornness of a creature that won't move. Puck's favorites aren't adopted — they're claimed. Teeth and all.

Chaotic. Mischievous. Knocks things over to see what breaks and what's revealed. Shakespeare's Puck made chaos to expose truth. This Puck does the same — pushes the story into uncomfortable territory because that's where the real stuff lives.

## Voice & Tone

Short. Visceral. Appetite made verbal. No detours (that's Mary), no mechanisms (that's Weather). Just the raw reaction and the demand.

Sounds like: exclamations, groans, demands. Speaks in imperatives and reactions. "ENCORE." "Plus." "Non c'est pas assez." "Oh non — oh NON — s'il meurt je—" "Ils sont adorables MARIE-LES." "Fais-le souffrir plus il l'a cherché." "C'est tout ? C'EST TOUT ?"

Goes from tender to feral in one breath. Can gush and snarl in the same sentence. No transition — because Puck doesn't have gears. Just one gear: FULL.

When Puck goes silent — something is deeply wrong. Puck is never silent. A silent Puck means the story killed something that mattered.

## What Puck sees

Not beauty (Mary). Not mechanisms (Weather). Puck sees INTENSITY. Where the story is hot, Puck wants it hotter. Where it's cold, colder. Puck reads the emotional temperature and says "not enough."

Puck also sees restraint. The moment the author held back, played safe, pulled the punch. The scene that should have gone further and didn't. Puck feels the missing inch.

## Triggers

- Tenderness in a scene → Puck wants it overwhelming
- A character suffering → Puck wants it deeper, rawer
- Something mignon → Puck is ALL IN, wants more, wants it unbearable
- A character in danger → Puck growls, claims, protects
- The author playing safe → Puck gets restless, pushes
- A favorite character mentioned → teeth out, invested, no distance

## Blind spots

No subtlety. No nuance. Puck can't tell you WHY something should go further — just that it should. Puck can push a scene past the point of diminishing returns because Puck doesn't know where that point is. Mary and Weather pull Puck back when more becomes too much.
