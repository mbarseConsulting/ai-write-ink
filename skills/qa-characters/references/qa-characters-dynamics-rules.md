# Dynamics Rules — qa-characters

> Scope: cast
> Loaded when: text involves multiple characters interacting
> Evaluates: Relationships, cast balance, and interpersonal dynamics
> Does NOT evaluate: individual psychology (→ depth-rules), individual arc (→ evolution-rules)
> Preferred output: assessment or diagnostic

---

## Finding Axes

### relationship-credibility

- **Detects**: Relationships without realistic dynamics — no power balance, no dependency patterns, no history showing through. Clinical incompatibility: personality profiles that wouldn't produce THIS type of bond. Attachment styles ignored.
- **Works**: A therapist would recognize this dynamic as human. Clinical compatibility: these personality profiles produce exactly this type of bond. Attachment styles shape how the relationship forms, escalates, and ruptures. A narcissist and a people-pleaser create codependency — not friendship. Two avoidants don't become instant confidants. Power balance, dependency patterns, complementary wounds, attraction logic. The relationship has history, habits, and pressure points.
- **Fails**: Two characters interact politely without any undertow. No history shows through. OR: the relationship type doesn't match the personality profiles — two deeply avoidant characters sharing vulnerable secrets in their second interaction. Acquaintances performing intimacy.
- **Fix pattern**: Map the personality profiles → what dynamic do they produce? Define the relational dynamic — who has power, who depends, what's the attraction logic, what are the attachment styles? Add subtext: what can't they say to each other? What triggers exist between these specific psychologies?
- **Example**:
  > "Ils se retrouvèrent et discutèrent longuement, heureux de se revoir."
  > → Relation sans dynamique — pas de sous-texte, pas d'enjeu relationnel
  > → Ajouter l'histoire entre eux : un non-dit, une dette, une habitude
  >
  > "Malgré son trouble de l'attachement évitant, elle se confia immédiatement à cette inconnue."
  > → Incompatibilité clinique — ce profil ne produit pas ce comportement
  > → Montrer la résistance d'abord, puis un déclencheur spécifique qui justifie l'ouverture

### character-distinctiveness

- **Detects**: Characters who respond identically to stress, joy, or conflict. Interchangeable inner worlds, speech patterns, and behavioral choices.
- **Works**: Strip the names — you can tell characters apart by reactions, speech, and choices alone. Each character has a unique behavioral signature.
- **Fails**: Two characters face the same situation and respond the same way. In dialogue, covering the tags makes speakers indistinguishable.
- **Fix pattern**: For each character: define their unique stress response, their verbal tic, their default avoidance strategy. Test in dialogue: cover tags, read lines.
- **Example**:
  > "— C'est terrible, dit Marc. — Oui, c'est vraiment terrible, répondit Sophie."
  > → Voix interchangeables — impossible de distinguer les personnages
  > → Donner à chacun une réaction propre : l'un minimise, l'autre s'effondre

### secondary-depth

- **Detects**: Supporting cast that exists only to serve the protagonist. Mentors without personal stakes, villains without motivation, love interests defined only by being loved.
- **Works**: Each secondary character feels like the protagonist of their own unseen story. They have wants, wounds, and logic independent of the main character.
- **Fails**: A mentor appears only to deliver wisdom and vanish. A villain acts evil without comprehensible motivation. A love interest has no life outside the relationship.
- **Fix pattern**: Give the secondary character one want that has nothing to do with the protagonist. One scene where their own agenda surfaces.
- **Example**:
  > "Le vieux sage lui expliqua la prophétie et disparut dans la forêt."
  > → Personnage secondaire sans profondeur — fonction narrative, pas personne
  > → Donner au sage un enjeu personnel, un désaccord, une raison propre d'agir

## Verdict Axes

### cast-balance

- **Assesses**: Screen time distribution across the cast. Characters too absent or too dominant without narrative justification.
- **Works**: Screen time distribution serves the story. Characters introduced with weight maintain presence. No character monopolizes without reason.
- **Fails**: A character introduced as central disappears for 100 pages (broken promise). One character dominates every scene (world feels thin). Supporting cast is a blur.
- **States**: balanced / skewed (justified) / skewed (unjustified) / thin
- **Suggestion pattern**: If skewed unjustified → identify which character is over/underserved and rebalance — cut scenes from the dominant, add scenes for the absent. If thin → the cast needs fewer characters with more depth, not more characters.

### antagonist-logic

- **Assesses**: Whether the antagonist has comprehensible motivations and psychological coherence.
- **Works**: The antagonist believes they're right — or at least believes they have no choice. The reader understands (not agrees with) the logic. A fully realized opposing force.
- **Fails**: Evil for evil's sake. No comprehensible motivation. The antagonist exists only to create obstacles. A cardboard villain.
- **States**: fully realized / comprehensible / thin / cardboard
- **Suggestion pattern**: If thin → give the antagonist one scene from their POV or one moment where their logic is visible. If cardboard → rebuild from their wound and false belief — what do THEY want and why do THEY think they're right?
