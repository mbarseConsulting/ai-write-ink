# Closing Rules — qa-reader

> Scope: bookends
> Loaded when: mode = bookends, text includes closing
> Evaluates: Last paragraph through last chapter — the pages that determine whether the reader recommends the book
> Preferred output: inline or diagnostic

---

## Finding Axes

### final-sentence-resonance

- **Detects**: Last sentences that just end instead of resonating. Flat closings, explanatory wrap-ups, accidental final lines. Also: last sentences that TRY too hard — overwrought, straining for profundity. The best endings feel effortless.
- **Works**: The last sentence lands. It echoes. The reader would remember it tomorrow. It compresses the book's emotional or thematic weight into minimum words. It doesn't explain — it resonates. Often the most powerful final sentences are simple and concrete.
- **Fails**: The last sentence is informational, decorative, or accidental. The book whimpers instead of landing. Or: overwrought metaphor, grandiose statement, purple prose. It tries to be the final sentence instead of naturally being it.
- **Fix pattern**: Cut everything after the resonant line. Concrete images resonate more than abstract statements. Short sentences land harder than long ones at the end. Often the true final sentence is buried two or three lines before the current ending.
- **Mechanism markers**: Final sentences that explain ("elle comprit alors que"), summarize ("ainsi se termina"), or describe routine actions ("elle ferma la porte et rentra chez elle"). The "quotation test": could this sentence be quoted alone and carry weight? Would a reader underline it? The "burial test": is the real ending two or three lines above the current one?
- **Genre note**: Thriller = sharp beat (revelation, twist, final threat). Romance = emotional note embodying the relationship's arrival. Literary = image or phrase reverberating with theme. Horror = unease — a door that stays open.
- **Example**:
  > "Et la vie continua comme avant."
  > → Platitude — la derniere phrase ne porte rien, ne resonne pas. C'est un haussement d'epaules, pas un atterrissage.
  > → "La vie continua — mais le silence, dans la maison, avait change de texture."

### emotional-precision

- **Detects**: Endings that hit the wrong emotional note. The most dramatic beat instead of the most true. Also: endings that aim for the "correct" emotion rather than the EARNED emotion.
- **Works**: The last beat is the RIGHT emotion for THIS story. Not the most dramatic — the most true. It feels inevitable even if unexpected. The ending delivers the emotion the story EARNED, which may not be the emotion the reader wanted.
- **Fails**: The ending goes for maximum drama when the story calls for quiet truth. Or ends on hope when the story earned ambiguity. Or: goes for the "correct" literary emotion (ambiguity, melancholy) regardless of what this specific story earned.
- **Fix pattern**: Ask: what emotion did this story earn? Not what would be satisfying — what is true given everything that happened. End there.
- **Mechanism markers**: The "earned test" — trace the emotional trajectory of the entire text. If the ending emotion diverges from that trajectory, it's hitting the wrong note. The "desire vs. logic test": does the ending match the author's DESIRE or the story's LOGIC? If desire, the reader feels the author's hand.
- **Genre note**: Romance = HEA/HFN must feel earned, not granted; barriers were deep, resolution must show emotional work. Thriller = release complicated by cost; protagonist survives, but at what price? Literary = often bittersweet, ambiguous, quiet; truth over catharsis. Horror = dread (continues), exhaustion (survival at cost), or haunted peace (over, but nothing is the same).
- **Example**:
  > (Histoire sombre et complexe se terminant sur une resolution nette et optimiste)
  > → Emotion finale en contradiction avec le ton du roman. The story earned ambiguity, delivered comfort.
  > → Aligner l'emotion de fin sur ce que l'histoire a construit, pas sur le confort. Si l'histoire parle de perte irreversible, la fin doit s'y tenir.

### closure-openness

- **Detects**: Accidental ambiguity (meant to close but didn't) or accidental closure (meant to leave open but resolved). Also: hedged endings — the author tries both, committing to neither.
- **Works**: A closed ending resolves. An open ending resonates. The reader can tell the choice was deliberate. Both can work — the choice must be intentional and executed cleanly.
- **Fails**: Ambiguous because the author didn't decide. Or a complex story gets a tidy bow it didn't earn. Or: the author hedges — suggesting resolution while keeping escape hatches. The reader feels the indecision.
- **Fix pattern**: Decide: closed or open. Commit fully. If open, the reader must have enough to construct valid interpretations. If closed, the resolution must feel earned. Half-measures are worse than either.
- **Mechanism markers**: The "intention test" — can you articulate what the open ending MEANS? Open endings give the reader enough for multiple valid interpretations. Accidental ambiguity just stops. The "bow test" — does the closed ending honor the story's complexity or simplify it?
- **Genre note**: Romance REQUIRES satisfying closure on the central relationship. Thriller requires central threat resolution. Literary earns the most latitude for open endings. Horror can end on openness (the threat remains) or closure (at cost).
- **Example**:
  > (Fin ou le conflit central n'est ni resolu ni deliberement laisse ouvert)
  > → Ambiguite accidentelle — ni resolu, ni volontairement ouvert. An open ending says "you decide." An unfinished ending says "I didn't."
  > → Choisir : fermer avec une resolution gagnee, ou ouvrir avec intention.

### climax-payoff

- **Detects**: Climaxes that don't deliver on the tension built. Underwhelming confrontations, deus ex machina, anticlimactic resolutions. Also: climaxes resolved through external intervention rather than character choice. And: climax resolving the EXTERNAL problem but not the INTERNAL one (or vice versa).
- **Works**: The highest-stakes moment feels like the consequence of everything before it. The protagonist resolves it through CHARACTER — through who they have become, through a choice only they could make. Every element was set up earlier. McKee: the gap between expectation and result is widest at the climax. The climax should resolve both external conflict AND internal arc.
- **Fails**: Resolved by coincidence, external intervention, or undiscovered power. Not proportional to the buildup. Or: protagonist passive in their own climax — saved rather than saving.
- **Fix pattern**: Trace backward — does every element have setup? Is the resolution earned by character choice, not chance? The character's internal transformation should enable the external resolution. The character's growth IS the key to resolution.
- **Mechanism markers**: The "setup audit" — list every element used in the climax. If any appears for the first time, it's deus ex machina. The "agency test" — is it resolved by protagonist's CHOICE or external force? The "proportion test" — is the climax proportional to the buildup? A 300-page build deserves more than a 3-page resolution.
- **Genre note**: Thriller = confrontation must match or exceed promised danger; underwhelming is fatal. Romance = the grand gesture proving internal barrier is overcome; surprising and inevitable. Literary = can be quiet but must carry the full weight; realization, moral choice, acceptance. Horror = confrontation with what was feared; reveal must maintain some mystery.
- **Example**:
  > (Antagoniste vaincu par un pouvoir que le protagoniste decouvre au dernier chapitre)
  > → Deus ex machina — le pouvoir n'est pas etabli, le climax n'est pas gagne. The reader didn't know this tool existed.
  > → Planter le pouvoir plus tot — setup en acte 1 ou 2. Les meilleurs climax font penser "bien sur — c'etait la depuis le debut."

### character-arrival

- **Detects**: Protagonist ending in the same internal state as the beginning. Transformation declared but not demonstrated. Also: transformation too neat — clean, resolved state that feels like fairy tale rather than human experience.
- **Works**: The protagonist has arrived somewhere different — internally. What they can see, do, or accept now that they couldn't at the beginning. The cost of change is visible. The best arrivals are bittersweet: something gained, something lost. Demonstrated through behavior, not narration.
- **Fails**: The character is the same at the end. Or "changed" only in narrated declaration. No visible cost. Or: complete, clean transformation with no ambiguity, no loss.
- **Fix pattern**: Show arrival through a scene echoing the opening — the character responds differently. The contrast IS the transformation. Show what the transformation COST.
- **Mechanism markers**: The "mirror test" — compare first scene and last scene. Do they respond to similar stimuli differently? If the character's last action could have been their first without contradiction, no arrival occurred. The "tell vs. show test" — is transformation DECLARED ("il avait change") or DEMONSTRATED (a choice impossible in chapter 1)?
- **Genre note**: Romance — both characters must arrive, changed by each other. Thriller — protagonist may be darker, wiser, haunted. Horror — survival itself may be the arrival. Literary — can be subtle, a shift in perception.
- **Example**:
  > "Il avait change. Il le savait. Tout serait different desormais."
  > → Transformation declaree, pas montree — le lecteur doit le VOIR, pas le lire.
  > → Le personnage qui ne pouvait pas s'excuser au chapitre 1 s'excuse au dernier — pas parce que le narrateur dit qu'il a change, mais parce que le lecteur le VOIT.

### resolution-pacing

- **Detects**: Post-climax too rushed (reader cheated) or too drawn out (reader disengages). Also: resolution introducing NEW conflicts — after the climax, the reader needs closure, not complication.
- **Works**: The resolution gives time to process without losing the reader. Space between climax and final page proportional to what needs settling. Each post-climax beat resolves one thread — no new threads opened. Close threads in order of decreasing importance: plot first, then character, then theme (final beat should be thematic).
- **Fails**: Climax 3 pages from the end — no room to land. Or 50-page resolution with no remaining tension. Or: new mystery or sequel hook that reopens settled business.
- **Fix pattern**: Map what MUST be resolved post-climax. Cut everything else. The reader needs a landing, not a second flight.
- **Mechanism markers**: The "necessity audit" — for each post-climax event: does the READER need this or does the AUTHOR? Cut everything the reader doesn't need. The "tension test" — once the main question is answered, is a secondary question sustaining the resolution, or is the reader just waiting for the book to end?
- **Genre note**: Romance needs resolution space — the reader wants to SEE the HEA/HFN. Thriller can end quickly after climax. Literary can take time — resolution IS meaning. Horror should end quickly — lingering dissipates dread.
- **Example**:
  > (Epilogue de 40 pages apres le climax, avec de multiples scenes de retrouvailles)
  > → Resolution trop longue — le lecteur a decroche apres le climax.
  > → Une scene montrant la nouvelle normalite. Une image qui resonne. Fin.

### last-image

- **Detects**: Endings without a strong final image. No visual or sensory impression to carry. Also: final images that are generic (sunset, horizon, rain on a window) rather than specific to THIS story.
- **Works**: The final image compresses the book's meaning into a single frame. It's what the reader carries after closing the book. Specific to this story — it couldn't end any other book. The best final images echo the opening image with a transformation.
- **Fails**: The ending is conceptual, abstract, or conversational. No image. Or: a stock image that could belong to any book. Or: image present but disconnected from themes.
- **Fix pattern**: Find the image that embodies the book's central truth. End on concrete, sensory. If possible, echo the opening image with a difference — the reader feels the distance traveled.
- **Mechanism markers**: The "frame test" — could this ending be a photograph? If it's a thought ("elle comprit"), a statement ("tout avait un sens"), or a dialogue line, there's no image. The "echo test" — does the final image connect to an image from earlier? The most powerful final images rhyme with the opening, now transformed. The "specificity test" — could this image end any other book? If yes, find a more specific one.
- **Example**:
  > "Elle comprit alors que tout avait un sens, finalement."
  > → Abstrait — pas d'image finale. Les pensees s'evaporent. Les images restent.
  > → "Le jardin, quand elle y revint, avait commence a pousser par-dessus la cloture."
