# Scene Rules — qa-reader

> Scope: meso
> Loaded when: text >= 1 complete scene
> Evaluates: Scene-level engagement — does the scene work as a unit of change?
> Preferred output: inline or diagnostic
> Traversal: Evaluate EVERY scene in the text, not just the most dramatic ones. Quiet scenes fail these axes as often as action scenes. If findings only appear for opening and closing scenes, go back and examine the middle ones.

---

## Finding Axes

### tension

- **Detects**: Scenes without stakes. Nothing at risk — no safety, trust, identity, secret, or relationship under pressure. The reader has no reason to wonder what happens next.
- **Works**: Something is at stake. The reader wants to know the outcome. Tension can be quiet (will she say it?) or loud (will he survive?). The engine is present. Tension operates on at least one level: physical, social, psychological, or moral.
- **Fails**: Characters interact pleasantly without friction. A meeting occurs but nothing is at risk. The scene could be removed and the reader would miss nothing. Or: stakes are present but invisible to the reader — the author knows what's at risk but hasn't made the reader feel it.
- **Fix pattern**: Identify what's at stake for the POV character. If nothing — add a want, a fear, or a secret. Make the stakes felt through physical or emotional reactions, not narrated explanation.
- **Mechanism markers**: Scenes where every character is comfortable, aligned, and satisfied. No wants in conflict. No information asymmetry. No ticking clock. The scene exists to convey information the author needs delivered, not to dramatize conflict. The "engine test": what question propels the reader through this scene? If there's no question, there's no engine.
- **Genre note**: Thriller = external stakes dominate (survival, deadline); scenes without a ticking element need character revelation to justify the pause. Romance = relational stakes (vulnerability, rejection, barrier); every scene should advance or complicate the central relationship. Literary = internal stakes (identity, moral choice, what remains unsaid); tension in what the character refuses to face. Horror = atmospheric stakes (safety eroding, normalcy cracking); tension from what's NOT shown.
- **Example**:
  > "Ils dejeunerent ensemble et discuterent de la pluie et du beau temps."
  > → Scene sans enjeu — rien en jeu, rien a resoudre. The reader has no question to propel them through the scene.
  > → Donner a un personnage un objectif cache. Sous la conversation banale, quelque chose doit bruler.

### scene-arc

- **Detects**: Scenes where nothing changes. The world is the same at the end as at the beginning — no relationship shift, no revelation, no decision, no belief cracking. McKee: a scene must shift at least one value charge. If the value is the same at start and end, it's not a scene.
- **Works**: The scene starts somewhere and ends somewhere different. Something shifts: a relationship, an understanding, a balance of power, a piece of knowledge. The value change is felt by the reader, not just logically present.
- **Fails**: Characters meet, talk, and separate without anything being different. No turn. It's a passage, not a scene.
- **Fix pattern**: Identify the turn. If there's no turn, add one: a revelation, a decision, a shift in power. Ask: what value is at stake? What charge at the start? At the end? If same — no scene.
- **Mechanism markers**: The "before/after test" — describe the story world before and after the scene. If identical, no arc. Scenes that are pure ambiance, pure information transfer, or pure interaction without movement. If removing the scene changes nothing downstream, the scene has no arc.
- **Example**:
  > (Entire scene of two characters catching up on news with no shift)
  > → Pas d'arc — les personnages sont au meme point au debut et a la fin.
  > → Introduire un basculement : une info change la donne, un non-dit eclate, le pouvoir bascule.

### scene-economy

- **Detects**: Scenes that start too early (arrival, greeting, settling in) or end too late (cooldown, departure, reflection on what just happened). Transitions that should be skipped. Also: scenes that could be summary instead of dramatized — not every event needs full treatment.
- **Works**: Start late, exit early. Enter at the moment of friction. Leave before the cooldown. The reader fills in transitions. The decision to dramatize vs. summarize is deliberate.
- **Fails**: The scene opens with parking, entering, greeting. The scene ends with goodbyes, departure, internal recap. Or: a routine event gets full dramatic treatment when a sentence of summary would do.
- **Fix pattern**: Cut approach and aftermath. Start at friction. End on the strongest beat. Mark the "real start" and "real end" — everything outside is fat.
- **Mechanism markers**: Opening actions with no dramatic value (entering rooms, sitting down, ordering drinks). Closing actions that decompress (leaving, driving home, thinking about what happened). The "fast-forward test": if a reader would skip ahead, you're starting too early or ending too late.
- **Genre note**: Literary fiction earns more approach time IF the approach carries interiority or sensory texture. Romance can earn a slower exit IF reflection deepens the emotional beat. Thriller gets almost no latitude.
- **Example**:
  > "Elle gara la voiture, verifia son maquillage dans le retroviseur, prit son sac et marcha vers l'entree du restaurant."
  > → Approche — quatre actions qui retardent l'engagement.
  > → Couper : "Elle s'assit en face de lui. Il n'avait pas touche a son verre."

### microtension

- **Detects**: Pages without micro-mysteries. No unanswered question, no detail that doesn't fit, no mismatch between what's said and done. No conflicting emotions within the POV character. The reader's unconscious engagement drops. Microtension is what keeps readers reading BETWEEN major plot points.
- **Works**: On every page: a question left open, a detail that intrigues, a gap between words and behavior. A character who smiles at the wrong moment. An object that doesn't belong. Also: conflicting emotions within the character — wanting and fearing the same thing. The friction of inner conflict sustains reading.
- **Fails**: A page of competent prose with no hooks. Everything makes sense. Nothing pulls forward. The reader can put the book down here. This is the most common failure in mid-manuscript passages.
- **Fix pattern**: Plant a small mystery on every page — a reaction that doesn't match, a detail the character notices but doesn't understand, an offhand remark with hidden weight. Give the POV character conflicting emotions. Not every micro-mystery needs resolution — some are texture. But the reader should never find a safe exit point.
- **Mechanism markers**: "Put-down zones" — stretches where no open question exists at any level. No gap between surface and depth. No conflicting emotions in the POV character. Prose is correct, scene functions, but nothing PULLS. The "safe exit test": could the reader stop here without any nagging feeling? If yes, microtension is missing.
- **Genre note**: Thriller = details that don't add up, surveillance anxiety, information the character can't yet interpret. Romance = mixed signals, gap between what characters say and what bodies do, attraction vs. resistance. Horror = wrongness — details almost normal, sounds that shouldn't be, the character's refusal to acknowledge what they sense. Literary = subtext, unacknowledged emotions, gap between self-narrative and what the reader sees.
- **Example**:
  > (Deux pages de conversation ou chaque declaration est prise au pied de la lettre)
  > → Aucune microtension — pas de question ouverte, pas de decalage.
  > → Un seul "Il sourit, mais pas des yeux" soutient une page entiere.

### character-agency

- **Detects**: POV character as passive observer. Things happen around them. They watch, react, are moved by events. No choices, no initiative, no action that drives the scene. Agency is not about action sequences — a character who DECIDES to remain silent has agency; a character who simply watches does not.
- **Works**: The POV character drives the scene. Makes choices. Takes action. Even in reactive scenes, they CHOOSE how to react. The reader follows a protagonist, not a passenger. Choices have consequences.
- **Fails**: The character watches a conversation between others. Events happen TO them. Decisions are made by other characters. The POV character is present but not active.
- **Fix pattern**: Give the character a decision to make. Replace observation with action. Even small choices (to stay, to speak, to lie, to look away) create agency. Ask: what does the character WANT in this scene?
- **Mechanism markers**: Count the POV character's verbs. If more than half are perception/cognition verbs (voir, entendre, sentir, penser, remarquer) without action verbs (dire, prendre, se lever, mentir, choisir), agency is failing. Three consecutive perception verbs without an action verb = red flag. The "passenger test": remove this character from the scene — does the scene still happen the same way? If yes, they have no agency.
- **Genre note**: In horror, controlled passivity works IF the character TRIES to act but is thwarted. In romance, agency means pursuing or resisting the relationship, not just having feelings.
- **Example**:
  > "Elle observa la dispute sans intervenir, se contentant d'ecouter les arguments de chacun."
  > → Passivite — le personnage regarde, le lecteur decroche.
  > → Lui donner un choix : intervenir, partir, mentir, choisir un camp. Meme choisir le silence doit etre un acte avec un cout visible.

### internal-stakes

- **Detects**: Scenes where external action occurs but the reader has no access to what it COSTS the POV character internally. Action without internal stakes is spectacle — it may be exciting but it doesn't resonate. Lisa Cron: the internal story is what gives the external story meaning.
- **Works**: The reader knows what the scene means to the character — what they stand to gain or lose emotionally, not just circumstantially. External events are filtered through internal experience. Internal and external stakes are intertwined.
- **Fails**: A fight scene where the reader knows the physical danger but not what the character fears beyond death. A conversation where the reader sees the social dynamics but not what the character needs from this encounter. Plot events unfold but the reader is watching from outside.
- **Fix pattern**: For each scene, answer: what does the POV character fear will happen? What do they want? What would it mean to them if they fail? Show internal stakes through physical sensation, intrusive thought, or behavior — not narrated explanation. One line of internal experience can transform a plot scene into a character scene.
- **Mechanism markers**: Action sequences where the character's emotional state is never shown. Scenes where the reader can describe what HAPPENED but not what the character FELT. The "cost test": if the character succeeds with no visible emotional cost, internal stakes are missing. The "remove test": remove the external events — is there still a story in the character's internal experience? If not, internal stakes are absent.
- **Example**:
  > (Sequence d'action : le personnage se bat, court, s'echappe — techniquement excitant mais emotionnellement plat)
  > → Enjeux externes sans enjeux internes — le lecteur voit l'action mais ne ressent pas le prix.
  > → Montrer ce que le personnage risque de perdre au-dela de sa vie : un lien, une promesse, sa propre image.

### motivation-reaction

- **Detects**: Stimulus-response sequences that feel "off" to the reader without them knowing why. Dwight Swain's Motivation-Reaction Units: external stimulus must come first, then the character responds in a natural order: (1) involuntary feeling or reflex, (2) rational action, (3) speech. Reversing this order feels wrong. Also: characters who react before the stimulus is presented to the reader.
- **Works**: The external event happens. The character feels it (body first). Then they act. Then they speak. The order feels natural. The reader experiences the event WITH the character.
- **Fails**: A character speaks a reaction before the reader has seen what they're reacting to. Or: a character acts rationally before showing any involuntary response to a shocking event. Or: a fully formed analytical thought before the body registers fear.
- **Fix pattern**: Reorder: stimulus first, then response (feel → act → speak). For shocking events, involuntary physical response must come before rational analysis.
- **Mechanism markers**: Reaction before stimulus ("— Non ! cria-t-elle. Puis elle vit le corps."). Rational analysis before physical reflex in high-stress moments. Speech before action in response to physical threat. The "sequence test": does the reader know WHAT happened before the character reacts to it? If the reader is confused about what triggered the reaction, the order is wrong.
- **Example**:
  > "— Non ! cria-t-elle. Puis elle vit le corps etendu sur le sol."
  > → Reaction avant stimulus — elle crie avant que le lecteur sache pourquoi.
  > → Inverser : "Le corps gisait sur le sol. Son cri lui echappa avant qu'elle comprenne."

### pacing

- **Detects**: Consecutive scenes at the same intensity level. All crisis, or all reflection. No alternation between tension and rest. The rhythm doesn't serve the story. Also: pacing that contradicts genre expectations, and sections where narrative speed doesn't match importance.
- **Works**: Tension and rest alternate. Acceleration before climax, breathing room after intensity. Important events get more narrative time, transitions get less. The reader is never exhausted or bored for sustained stretches.
- **Fails**: Three consecutive high-tension scenes (reader exhausted). Three consecutive reflective scenes (reader bored). Or: a pivotal confrontation told in two paragraphs while a minor errand gets three pages.
- **Fix pattern**: Map scene energy across the sequence. Insert a contrasting scene where intensity flatlines. Ensure narrative time is proportional to dramatic importance.
- **Mechanism markers**: Create a simple energy map: H (high), M (medium), L (low) for each scene. Three consecutive same-letter entries = pacing problem. Scene LENGTH should generally correlate with intensity: if a low-energy scene is the longest in the sequence, it's dragging.
- **Genre note**: Thriller = high baseline pace with brief breathing beats; long reflective passages are deadly. Romance = push-pull rhythm between intimacy and conflict; the alternation IS the pacing. Horror = slow build with punctuated shocks; long normalcy makes breaks devastating. Literary = slower baseline earned through language and interiority, but sustained stasis is boring, not literary.
- **Example**:
  > (Trois scenes de combat/poursuite consecutives sans temps mort)
  > → Rythme — trois scenes haute tension consecutives, epuisement lecteur.
  > → Inserer un temps de respiration — qui FAIT quelque chose (moment de personnage, revelation), pas juste une pause.

### pov-strategy

- **Detects**: POV choices that undermine the reading experience. Wrong POV character for this scene, narrative distance that doesn't serve the emotional need, POV shifts that disorient without purpose. Also: POV character who conveniently "doesn't notice" something obvious, to preserve a surprise for the reader.
- **Works**: The POV character is the one with the most at stake. Narrative distance matches emotional intensity. POV shifts are motivated. Information control flows naturally from what the POV character would notice.
- **Fails**: A scene told from the bystander's POV. Distant narration during emotional climax. POV switches mid-chapter without earning the shift. POV character conveniently blind to the obvious.
- **Fix pattern**: Identify whose scene this is — who has the most to lose? Tell it from their eyes. If the POV character would naturally notice something, let them — find another way to create surprise.
- **Mechanism markers**: "Witness scenes" — the most interesting thing happens to someone NOT the POV character. Distance mismatch — emotional climax in distant third ("elle etait triste") instead of close third. POV shifts where the reader needs more than two sentences to reorient. The "stake test": is the POV character the one with the most to lose in this scene? If not, consider switching.
- **Genre note**: Romance often uses dual POV — the reader NEEDS both perspectives. Thriller uses POV to control information — villain's POV creates dramatic irony. Literary stays deep in single POV for sustained interiority.
- **Example**:
  > (Confrontation climactique racontee du point de vue d'un personnage secondaire qui regarde de loin)
  > → POV mal choisi — le lecteur est a distance de l'enjeu central.
  > → Raconter depuis le personnage qui a le plus a perdre dans cette scene.

### temporal-effect

- **Detects**: Temporal structure choices that weaken engagement. Flashbacks that kill momentum, non-linear chronology that doesn't earn its complexity, time jumps that skip the interesting part. Also: flashbacks triggered by "remembering" — the most inert form of temporal shift.
- **Works**: Flashbacks arrive when the reader needs them — not when the author wants to explain. Non-linear structure creates a mystery the reader wants to solve. Time jumps land on the interesting side. Every temporal departure earns its return.
- **Fails**: A flashback interrupts a tense scene. Non-linear chronology creates confusion without mystery. A time jump skips the confrontation and shows the aftermath. A flashback triggered by "remembering" — the most inert form.
- **Fix pattern**: For flashbacks: does the reader need this NOW, or does the author? For non-linear: questions or confusion? For time jumps: skipping the boring part (good) or the interesting part (bad)?
- **Mechanism markers**: "Interrupt test" — is the flashback interrupting active tension? "Information test" — does the flashback answer a question the reader was ASKING (good) or one the author wants to deliver (bad)? "Landing test" — after a time jump, does the narrative land on higher or lower energy than what was skipped?
- **Genre note**: Thrillers use flashbacks sparingly — momentum is sacred. Literary fiction earns non-linear structure through thematic resonance. Horror uses temporal displacement for dread. Romance uses flashbacks to reveal history behind current barriers.
- **Example**:
  > (Flashback d'enfance insere entre le moment ou un personnage sort une arme et le denouement)
  > → Flashback qui casse le momentum — le lecteur veut savoir ce qui se passe, pas pourquoi.
  > → Deplacer le flashback avant la confrontation, ou apres la resolution.
