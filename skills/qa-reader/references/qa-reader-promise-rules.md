# Promise Rules — qa-reader

> Scope: bookends (macro)
> Loaded when: mode = bookends, text includes both opening and closing
> Evaluates: The macro connection between opening and closing — did the book keep its promises?
> Preferred output: assessment

Requires both beginning and end to evaluate.

---

## Verdict Axes

### narrative-promise

- **Assesses**: What kind of book the opening promised. Was the promise clear? Was it specific enough to differentiate from others in the genre?
- **Works**: The opening establishes a clear contract: what kind of experience the reader is signing up for. Specific enough to differentiate this book from others in its genre. General enough to allow surprises within the frame.
- **Fails**: The reader finishes the opening without knowing what kind of book they're reading. Or the promise is clear but misleading. Or so generic it could apply to any book in the genre.
- **States**: clear / muddled / misleading
- **Suggestion pattern**: If muddled → sharpen the opening to signal the dominant experience. If misleading → realign or ensure the shift is itself compelling. A broken promise can work if what's delivered is BETTER.
- **Diagnostic method**: Describe the book the opening promises in one sentence. Then the book delivered in one sentence. If different sentences: surprising and delightful = subversion; confusing or disappointing = misleading.

### genre-contract

- **Assesses**: Whether the book delivers what the genre reader expects to FEEL — not just genre tropes (surface), but the core emotional experience the genre exists to provide.
- **Works**: Genre conventions honored or subverted with something better. The genre reader gets the emotional experience they came for, even if the path surprises. The how can surprise — the what must deliver. Subversion works only when the alternative is MORE satisfying.
- **Fails**: Conventions broken without compensation. Or followed so rigidly the book has no identity. Or: broken accidentally rather than deliberately — the author unaware of the conventions they're violating.
- **States**: honored / subverted (successfully) / broken
- **Suggestion pattern**: If broken → identify which convention was violated and what the reader lost. Either restore or exceed. If too rigid → let the story's uniqueness inflect the conventions.
- **Genre-specific contracts**:
  - *Romance*: HEA or HFN is non-negotiable. The central couple must end together. Breaking this breaks the genre.
  - *Thriller*: Central threat must be resolved. Protagonist must be agent of resolution.
  - *Horror*: The horror must be confronted — it can win, but it must be faced.
  - *SF/Fantasy*: World rules must be internally consistent. The speculative premise must matter — can't be decorative.
  - *Mystery*: The puzzle must have a fair solution — clues the reader could have assembled.
  - *Literary*: The contract is with voice and depth. Generic prose after a brilliant opening is a violation.
  - *Historical*: Period must feel lived-in, not researched. Maintain the immersion standard set by opening.
- **Diagnostic method**: Identify the genre's core emotional promise (thriller = sustained tension/release, romance = emotional connection + payoff, literary = depth, horror = dread/uncanny). Did the reader GET that feeling? If yes, contract holds regardless of unconventional path.

### thematic-arc

- **Assesses**: Whether the theme is present in embryo at the start and answered (or deliberately left open) at the end. The thematic arc as a conversation between beginning and end.
- **Works**: The closing feels like the book's argument has been made — through story, not statement. The position may be ambiguous but must be deliberate. The story's EVENTS argue the theme — characters don't need to state it.
- **Fails**: Theme absent from the opening, or present but abandoned by closing. Or: stated rather than demonstrated. Or: the thematic position at the end contradicts what the events demonstrated.
- **States**: complete / present but unresolved / absent at start or end
- **Suggestion pattern**: If unresolved → ensure closing takes a position through action or image, even ambiguous. If absent at start → plant the seed through situation, not statement. If absent at end → a moment where the story's argument crystallizes through action or image, not narration.
- **Diagnostic method**: The thematic question should be implicit in the opening scene (through situation) and answered by the closing scene (through action). If you can trace the line from opening to closing and articulate the argument, the arc works. If the theme only appears in the middle, it's grafted on, not structural.

### promise-fulfillment

- **Assesses**: Whether the ending delivers the contract made in the opening. The ratio of promise to payoff must feel proportional.
- **Works**: Promise and payoff proportional. The payoff exceeds the promise in depth if not in spectacle. McKee's gap: the payoff is both surprising and inevitable — different from expected but more satisfying.
- **Fails**: The opening writes a check the ending can't cash. Or the ending delivers something the opening never hinted at. Or: proportional but predictable — exactly what was promised, no more.
- **States**: delivered / partially / betrayed
- **Suggestion pattern**: If partially → identify which promise was underfulfilled; strengthen the payoff. If betrayed → scale back the opening's promise or scale up the ending's delivery.
- **Diagnostic method**: List the implicit promises of the opening (not plot setup, but READING EXPERIENCE promises — intimacy? spectacle? devastation? challenge?). For each: does the closing deliver? Partial delivery usually means the easiest promise was kept but the deepest one was dodged.

### arc-completion

- **Assesses**: Whether all major character and plot arcs reach a satisfying state. Not whether the character transformed, but whether the reader feels the arcs are resolved, evolved, or deliberately left open.
- **Works**: Every arc resolved, evolved, or deliberately open. Every thread the reader tracked is acknowledged. Resolution doesn't mean happy ending — it means the arc reached a resting point the reader can accept. Threads that got significant page time resolved with proportional page time.
- **Fails**: A central arc never resolved. A plot thread from act 1 vanishes. A subplot resolved with a throwaway line that doesn't respect the reader's investment.
- **States**: complete / mostly / threads abandoned
- **Suggestion pattern**: If mostly → list incomplete threads, resolve or acknowledge deliberately. If abandoned → cut the setup or add the payoff. Every thread that gets significant page time must be resolved with proportional page time.
- **Diagnostic method**: The "thread inventory" — list every thread the reader tracks at the midpoint. For each: resolved, evolved, open (deliberately), or abandoned. Abandoned = worst outcome, signals the author lost track.

### surprise-inevitability

- **Assesses**: Whether the ending feels both unexpected and the only possible conclusion. McKee's ultimate test: the gap between expectation and result should be widest at the climax, yet every element was in place.
- **Works**: The reader didn't see it coming, but looking back, it was the only possible ending. Seeds planted in act 1 bloom in ways that feel both fresh and prepared. The reader WANTS to reread.
- **Fails**: Predictable (reader saw it from page 1). Or arbitrary (ending from nowhere with no setup). Or: surprising but not inevitable — a twist that shocks but doesn't hold up to scrutiny.
- **States**: inevitable-and-surprising / predictable / arbitrary
- **Suggestion pattern**: If predictable → add one element that reframes. The reader should say "of COURSE" not "I knew it." If arbitrary → plant seeds that make it earned. If surprising-but-not-inevitable → add subtle foreshadowing discoverable on reread.
- **Diagnostic method**: The "reread test" — would a rereader find hidden setups making the ending feel inevitable? If yes, invisible architecture. If nothing was missed, either too obvious (predictable) or no seeds (arbitrary). The best endings reward the rereader with "I should have seen it."
