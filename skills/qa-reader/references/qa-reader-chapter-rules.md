# Chapter Rules — qa-reader

> Scope: meso
> Loaded when: text >= 1 complete chapter
> Evaluates: Chapter-level engagement — the chapter as a unit within the larger structure
> Preferred output: diagnostic or assessment
> Traversal: Evaluate EVERY chapter in the text. If the text has 10 chapters, produce findings for ALL 10, even if some are brief. Dead zones hide in chapters you skip.

---

## Finding Axes

### chapter-ending

- **Detects**: Chapter endings that fail to create forward momentum. Summaries, fade-outs, neat resolutions that don't push the reader into the next chapter. Also: artificial cliffhangers that feel manipulative — shock for shock's sake, not earned by the scene.
- **Works**: The last paragraph creates a reason to turn the page: a question, a revelation, a reversal, a decision. The best endings feel inevitable AND create a new question. Character-driven hooks ("I need to know what she decides") outlast plot-driven ones ("I need to know what exploded"). The chapter ending is the second most important sentence after the opening.
- **Fails**: The chapter ends on a summary, tidy resolution, or character going to sleep. No forward pull. Or: a manufactured cliffhanger that betrays the scene's organic rhythm.
- **Fix pattern**: End on an open beat. Cut the last paragraph if it's cooldown. For chapters that naturally resolve, end on the IMPLICATION — what does this change mean for what comes next?
- **Mechanism markers**: Final paragraphs containing: sleep/rest actions, emotional resolution statements ("il se sentit enfin en paix"), summary/recap ("cette journee avait ete longue"), temporal closure ("le soleil se coucha"). The "question test": does the last sentence create a question or close one? If it closes, cut to the sentence before. The "permission test": does the ending give the reader permission to stop? Sleep, summary, and resolution all say "you can stop here."
- **Genre note**: Thriller = every ending MUST create urgency; cliffhangers, revelations, new threats — "just one more chapter" is the genre contract. Romance = end on emotional open beat (connection interrupted, vulnerability exposed, misunderstanding born). Literary = can end on resonance rather than urgency, but must still pull forward, even quietly. Horror = end on wrongness confirmed — something noticed but dismissed, something the reader understands that the character doesn't.
- **Example**:
  > "Epuisee, elle se coucha et s'endormit aussitot."
  > → Fin de chapitre sur l'endormissement — aucun elan vers la suite. Le sommeil est une cloture narrative.
  > → "Elle ferma les yeux — mais le bruit, elle en etait sure maintenant, venait de l'interieur des murs."

### emotional-truth

- **Detects**: Emotional beats that ring false to the reader. Emotions that feel convenient, unearned, or disconnected from what the scene built. Also: emotional escalation that skips steps (zero to devastation without intermediates) and emotional flatness where intensity is warranted.
- **Works**: The reader recognizes the emotion as true, even if surprising. Complex emotions are more believable than simple ones — a character who feels relief AND guilt simultaneously reads as more true than one who feels only guilt. Joy and grief coexist. Anger masks fear. The emotional beat feels earned by what came before.
- **Fails**: The reader feels the author's hand forcing the emotion. A forgiveness that comes too easily. A devastation that seems manufactured. Or: an emotion arrives at full intensity without the scene building toward it. Emotions conveniently serving the plot's next requirement.
- **Fix pattern**: Identify what the scene built emotionally. If the emotion jumps ahead, add the missing steps — resistance before surrender, confusion before clarity. Allow contradictory emotions: they read as more true than clean, simple ones.
- **Mechanism markers**: Emotion jumps — state A to state C without state B. Speed of emotional transition (instant forgiveness, sudden love, zero-to-rage). The "earn test": has the scene shown enough for this emotion to land? The "convenience test": does this emotion arrive because the character FEELS it, or because the PLOT needs it next?
- **Genre note**: Romance demands particularly precise emotional beats — false notes are fatal; the reader is there FOR the emotions. Literary fiction: emotional truth IS the project. In thriller, emotional truth matters less for plot scenes but is critical in character-revealing moments.
- **Example**:
  > "Malgre tout ce qu'il lui avait fait, elle lui pardonna instantanement."
  > → Emotion qui ne passe pas — le pardon n'est pas gagne par la scene.
  > → Montrer la resistance, le conflit interieur — le moment ou le pardon devient possible, puis le moment ou il devient reel.

### information-reveal

- **Detects**: Poorly timed revelations. Too early kills suspense. Too late frustrates. Also: reveals that don't create new questions — once answered, engagement drops. And: exposition delivered through unnatural dialogue ("as you know, Bob" — characters telling each other things they both already know, for the reader's benefit). McKee: exposition through conflict is invisible; exposition through dialogue is a lecture.
- **Works**: What the chapter reveals and withholds is deliberate. Each chapter changes what the reader knows — and what they THINK they know. Every major reveal opens a new question — the answer becomes a door, not a wall. Information arrives through scene action and conflict, not through characters narrating facts.
- **Fails**: A crucial secret revealed in chapter 2 that should sustain until act 3. Or: basic orientation withheld for 50 pages with no payoff. Or: a reveal answers everything, leaving no forward questions. Or: "as you know, Bob" dialogue.
- **Fix pattern**: Map what the reader knows vs. needs. Delay or advance to maximize impact. Every reveal should create a new question. Deliver information through conflict and action, not characters explaining to each other.
- **Mechanism markers**: "Suspense audit" — for each major piece: when planted, when revealed, what new question does the reveal create? A reveal that closes without opening = engagement drop. A question held open 3+ chapters without progress = patience thinning. The "Bob test": are characters telling each other things they both know?
- **Genre note**: Thriller = information control IS the genre; each chapter shifts understanding; plant false certainties, then shatter. Romance = emotional revelations (backstory, fears, vulnerabilities) at the right moment; don't reveal the wound before the reader cares. Horror = what's NOT revealed is as important as what is; over-explanation kills horror. SF/Fantasy = worldbuilding reveals must be woven into dramatic moments.
- **Example**:
  > "— Tu sais bien que notre pere est mort pendant la guerre, et que c'est pour ca que notre mere..."
  > → "As you know, Bob" — personnages s'expliquant des faits connus.
  > → Reveler par la friction : un desaccord sur l'heritage, une photo trouvee, un silence eloquent.

### scene-sequel-balance

- **Detects**: Chapters where the balance between action scenes (goal-conflict-disaster) and reflective sequels (reaction-dilemma-decision) is off. Dwight Swain: after intense scenes, the character needs processing time (sequel) before the next push (new scene). Missing either half creates specific reader problems.
- **Works**: After a major event, the character has time to react, process, and decide before moving forward. After reflective passages, the character sets a new goal and acts. The proportions vary by genre: thrillers have shorter sequels, literary fiction has longer ones.
- **Fails**: Three consecutive action scenes with no processing time — the reader feels rushed and stops caring. Or: extended reflection with no decision — the character stalls. Worst form: devastating experience followed immediately by next action with zero visible processing.
- **Fix pattern**: After intense scenes, add a sequel beat: feel → dilemma → decision. After reflective passages, push toward a decision that launches new action. The sequel doesn't need to be a full scene — a paragraph or even a sentence of internal response can suffice.
- **Mechanism markers**: Character witnesses major event and launches next action with no emotional transition. Extended interiority with no decision at the end. The "processing test": after each major scene, does the character have a visible reaction before their next action? How many words between the most intense scene and the character's emotional response? Zero = sequel missing. The "rhythm test": could you draw a scene-sequel-scene-sequel pattern? If it's scene-scene-scene, sequels are missing.
- **Genre note**: Thrillers have shorter sequels (a breath, not a chapter). Literary fiction has longer ones. Romance needs emotional processing after every major relationship beat.
- **Example**:
  > (Le personnage assiste a la mort d'un ami au chapitre 7, puis ouvre le chapitre 8 en poursuivant une nouvelle piste sans traitement emotionnel)
  > → Sequel manquant — le personnage n'a pas absorbe ce qui vient de se passer, le lecteur non plus.
  > → Ouvrir le chapitre 8 avec la reaction : choc, deni, colere — puis la decision d'agir MALGRE.

## Verdict Axes

### necessity

- **Assesses**: Whether the chapter justifies its existence. Remove it — what's lost?
- **Works**: The chapter does at least TWO of: advance plot, shift character, deliver essential information, create needed experience. Its removal would leave a visible gap.
- **Fails**: Nothing of consequence disappears if cut. Or: the chapter does only one thing that could be absorbed by a neighboring chapter.
- **States**: essential / supporting / redundant / cuttable
- **Suggestion pattern**: If supporting → strengthen the weak function. If redundant → merge with adjacent chapter. If cuttable → cut and redistribute any necessary information.
- **Mechanism markers**: The "gap test" — summarize the story with this chapter removed. If the summary is identical, it's cuttable. If it has a minor gap easily filled, it's redundant. If something important is lost, it's at least supporting.
