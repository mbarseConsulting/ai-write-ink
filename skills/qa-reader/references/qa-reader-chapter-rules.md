# Chapter Rules — qa-reader

> Scope: meso
> Loaded when: text >= 1 complete chapter
> Evaluates: Chapter-level engagement — the chapter as a unit within the larger structure
> Preferred output: diagnostic or assessment

---

## Finding Axes

### chapter-ending

- **Detects**: Chapter endings that fail to create forward momentum. Summaries, fade-outs, neat resolutions that don't push the reader into the next chapter.
- **Works**: The last paragraph creates a reason to turn the page: a question, a revelation, a reversal, a decision, an arrival. The chapter ending is the second most important sentence after the opening.
- **Fails**: The chapter ends on a summary of what happened. A tidy emotional resolution. A description of the character going to sleep. No forward pull.
- **Fix pattern**: End on an open beat — a question unanswered, a door opened, a revelation that demands consequences. Cut the last paragraph if it's cooldown.
- **Example**:
  > "Épuisée, elle se coucha et s'endormit aussitôt."
  > → Fin de chapitre sur l'endormissement — aucun élan vers la suite
  > → Couper avant le sommeil, finir sur la dernière pensée irrésolue

### emotional-truth

- **Detects**: Emotions that feel plot-mandated rather than psychologically earned. Characters feeling what the story needs rather than what their psychology demands. Convenient emotional beats.
- **Works**: Emotions feel earned, human, and contradictory. Joy and grief coexist. Anger masks fear. The reader recognizes the emotion as true, even if surprising.
- **Fails**: A character forgives too easily because the plot needs reconciliation. A character is devastated by something they wouldn't care about. The emotional beat serves structure, not character.
- **Fix pattern**: Root the emotion in character psychology. What would THIS person actually feel in THIS situation, given their history? Allow contradictory feelings.
- **Example**:
  > "Malgré tout ce qu'il lui avait fait, elle lui pardonna instantanément."
  > → Émotion au service du plot — le pardon n'est pas gagné
  > → Montrer la résistance, le conflit intérieur, avant tout basculement

### information-reveal

- **Detects**: Poorly timed revelations. Information delivered too early (kills suspense) or too late (frustrates). Information the reader needs to engage withheld beyond the patience threshold.
- **Works**: What the chapter reveals and withholds is deliberate. Each chapter changes what the reader knows — and what they THINK they know. Timing serves tension.
- **Fails**: A crucial secret is revealed in chapter 2 that should have sustained tension until act 3. Or the reader lacks basic orientation information for 50 pages with no payoff for the confusion.
- **Fix pattern**: Map what the reader knows vs. what they need. Delay or advance the reveal to maximize dramatic impact. Every reveal should create a new question.
- **Example**:
  > (Character reveals the central mystery's answer in casual dialogue, chapter 3)
  > → Révélation prématurée — le mystère central est résolu trop tôt
  > → Retarder : donner un indice partiel ici, garder la révélation pour le climax

## Verdict Axes

### necessity

- **Assesses**: Whether the chapter justifies its existence. Remove it — what's lost?
- **Works**: The chapter advances plot, shifts a character, delivers essential information, or creates an experience the reader needs. Its removal would leave a visible gap.
- **Fails**: Nothing of consequence disappears if the chapter is cut. No plot advancement, no character shift, no essential information. The chapter is filler.
- **States**: essential / supporting / redundant / cuttable
- **Suggestion pattern**: If supporting → identify which function is weak and strengthen it. If redundant → merge with adjacent chapter. If cuttable → cut and redistribute any necessary information.
