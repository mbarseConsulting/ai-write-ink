# Micro Rules — qa-reader

> Scope: micro
> Loaded when: always
> Evaluates: Paragraph and passage level engagement — the reader's moment-to-moment experience
> Preferred output: inline
> Traversal: Apply to EVERY page/section of the text, not just salient passages. If micro findings only appear in the first third, re-examine the middle and end.

---

## Finding Axes

### hook

- **Detects**: First sentence of scene/chapter that fails to create a question, an image, or a voice. Openings that explain, set up context, describe weather, or orient before engaging.
- **Works**: The first sentence is an audition. It creates curiosity, plants an image, or establishes a voice the reader wants to follow. Not a gimmick — a promise. The best hooks do triple duty: engage, establish voice, and orient.
- **Fails**: The scene opens with exposition, weather description, or neutral context-setting. Nothing pulls the reader forward. The first line could be cut without losing engagement. Or: the hook is a gimmick — shock value disconnected from the scene's actual content.
- **Fix pattern**: Replace with a line that creates a question, plants a sensory image, or introduces a voice with attitude. Enter the scene at the point of interest. The hook must be honest — it should promise what the scene delivers.
- **Mechanism markers**: Temporal orienting ("ce matin-la"), weather openings, meta-narrative ("l'histoire commence"), context-setting ("depuis trois ans"), empty state descriptions ("tout etait calme"). The "necessity test": does this sentence make the next sentence NECESSARY? If the reader can skip it, it fails.
- **Genre note**: Thriller = danger or mystery. Literary = voice worth following. Romance = chemistry or longing. Horror = wrongness beneath the ordinary. SF/Fantasy = lived-in detail implying the world.
- **Example**:
  > "Il faisait froid ce matin-la et le ciel etait gris."
  > → Ouverture meteo — aucune question, aucune tension, aucune voix. The sentence informs but creates no need for the next sentence.
  > → Entrer par un geste, un detail etrange, ou une pensee qui accroche.

### sentence-rhythm

- **Detects**: Monotonous sentence patterns — same length, same structure, same cadence across consecutive sentences. Rhythm that contradicts the scene's emotional register (long, languid sentences during a chase; staccato during a love scene).
- **Works**: Sentence length and structure vary to create music. Short sentences punch. Long sentences immerse. The rhythm matches the emotional register: staccato for tension, flowing for reflection, fragmented for shock. Paragraph breaks create breathing room.
- **Fails**: Five consecutive sentences of identical length and structure. The prose sounds like a metronome. Or: rhythm fights the content — a chase scene in subordinate clauses, an intimate moment chopped into telegraphs.
- **Fix pattern**: Vary sentence length deliberately. After a long sentence, a short one lands harder. Match rhythmic energy to emotional energy. Read aloud — the ear catches what the eye misses.
- **Mechanism markers**: Count consecutive sentences of similar length. Three or more = monotony risk. Check for repeated syntactic patterns (SVO chains). The "metronome test": does every sentence land on the same beat? Exception: deliberate repetition for rhetorical effect (anaphora) is a feature, not a flaw.
- **Example**:
  > "Elle ouvrit la porte. Elle regarda a droite. Elle regarda a gauche. Elle avanca dans le couloir. Elle s'arreta devant la chambre."
  > → Cinq phrases SVO identiques — effet metronomique, le lecteur decroche.
  > → Varier : fusionner des actions en une phrase longue, puis couper net. "Elle poussa la porte, balaya le couloir du regard — vide. La chambre, au fond."

### word-precision

- **Detects**: Approximate word choices where a precise one exists. Cliches and dead metaphors ("un froid glacial", "un silence de mort"). Abstract nouns where concrete ones would land. Adverb-adjective padding that substitutes for the right verb or noun. Filter words (il vit, elle entendit, il sentit) that place the reader outside the perception instead of inside it.
- **Works**: Every word is irreplaceable. Verbs carry specificity (not "elle marcha" but "elle rasait les murs"). Metaphors are alive. The author's word choices reveal a mind at work, not on autopilot.
- **Fails**: Generic vocabulary: "beau", "grand", "petit", "triste", "heureux" where a more precise word exists. Adverb stacking ("elle courut rapidement") where a better verb would do. Filter verbs that distance the reader from perception.
- **Fix pattern**: Replace the generic with the specific. Kill the adverb, upgrade the verb. Cut filter words — go straight to the perception. Ask: is this the ONLY word that works here?
- **Mechanism markers**: High-frequency vague verbs (faire, aller, etre, avoir, mettre). Adjectives that are category labels, not sensory descriptions. Metaphors that appear in 100+ novels. Filter verb chains (il vit, elle entendit, il sentit que). The "visibility test": can the reader SEE it? If the word is a container without content ("geste", "air", "chose"), it fails.
- **Example**:
  > "Elle fit un geste etrange et le regarda d'un air bizarre."
  > → Three words that should carry the sentence are empty containers. The reader constructs nothing.
  > → Le geste precis : "Elle retourna lentement le couteau, pointe vers elle, et le posa entre eux."

### density

- **Detects**: Passages that can be removed without loss. Throat-clearing openings, redundant transitions, scenes restating what's established. Also: consecutive sentences saying the same thing differently.
- **Works**: Every passage earns its place. Each paragraph advances the scene, deepens a character, or creates needed texture. The text trusts the reader — it says things once.
- **Fails**: A paragraph establishes something already clear. Two consecutive sentences say the same thing ("Il etait epuise. La fatigue pesait sur ses epaules."). Remove the passage — nothing changes.
- **Fix pattern**: Cut or merge into a stronger neighbor. If two sentences say the same thing, kill the weaker one. Trust the reader to remember.
- **Mechanism markers**: The "delete test" — can this paragraph be removed without the reader losing anything? If yes, it fails. Recap openings ("comme on l'a vu"), restated emotions, repeated atmospheric descriptions. Two-sentence redundancy: both say the same thing — one must go.
- **Genre note**: SF/Fantasy and Historical get more latitude for worldbuilding — but only through character perception, not encyclopedic narration. Thriller gets almost none — every paragraph must drive forward.
- **Example**:
  > "Il etait epuise. La fatigue pesait sur ses epaules comme une chape de plomb. Il n'en pouvait plus."
  > → Trois phrases pour une seule idee — redondance interne.
  > → Garder la plus forte (la deuxieme, image concrete), couper les deux autres.

### sensory-clarity

- **Detects**: Scenes without sensory grounding. Abstract atmosphere ("the room was tense") instead of concrete detail. Over-reliance on a single sense (usually sight). Also: sensory overload — too many details competing for attention. Missing physical reality.
- **Works**: The reader can see, hear, feel the scene. At least two senses per scene anchor. Sensory details are filtered through the POV character's perception — a chef notices smells, a musician hears pitch, a fighter reads exits. The hierarchy of senses reveals character. Selective, not exhaustive.
- **Fails**: The scene happens in a void. Emotions are named but not felt through the body. Or: every detail is visual — the scene is a photograph, not a lived experience. Or: more than three sensory details in a single paragraph = overload risk.
- **Fix pattern**: Add one specific sensory detail that grounds the scene. Use non-visual senses for emotional scenes: touch and sound create intimacy; smell triggers memory; taste grounds visceral moments. Anchor through what the POV character perceives first.
- **Mechanism markers**: Abstract emotion words without physical correlate ("tension", "malaise", "joie"). Scenes with dialogue but no spatial anchoring. Long passages with zero sensory verbs. The "void test": close your eyes — can you hear anything in this scene? Feel any temperature? If the scene is silent and temperatureless, it's a void.
- **Genre note**: Horror relies on sensory wrongness — a smell that shouldn't be there, a sound that stops too abruptly. Romance needs the sensory register of physical awareness between characters. SF/Fantasy needs details that build the world without explaining it.
- **Example**:
  > "La tension entre eux etait devenue insupportable."
  > → Abstrait — rien de physique. "Tension" est un label, pas une experience.
  > → Montrer par un son, un geste, un objet : le tintement d'une fourchette posee trop soigneusement, une chaise reculee de deux centimetres.

### showing-vs-telling

- **Detects**: The author names an emotion or state instead of rendering it through action, dialogue, or sensory detail. Also detects R.U.E. violations (Browne & King: Resist the Urge to Explain): showing the emotion effectively AND THEN explaining it — the show-then-tell double tap that kills the effect. A subtler form: telling through internal monologue that restates what the scene already demonstrated.
- **Works**: The reader FEELS the emotion through concrete evidence. The author trusts the reader to interpret. "She slammed the door" is enough — adding "She was furious." kills it. The reader's inference is more powerful than the author's declaration.
- **Fails**: Named emotions: "il etait en colere", "elle se sentit triste". Named character traits: "Marie etait courageuse." Named atmosphere: "l'ambiance etait etrange." Show-then-tell double tap: showing followed by explaining.
- **Fix pattern**: Delete the telling. If the showing is already there, the telling is redundant. If there's only telling, replace with concrete behavior, physical sensation, or dialogue. Ask: can the reader figure out the emotion from the evidence?
- **Mechanism markers**: Named emotion verbs (sentir, ressentir, eprouver + emotion noun). Character trait declarations (X etait + adjective). The "R.U.E. double-tap test": is the sentence after a strong showing moment an EXPLANATION of what was just shown? If the physical/behavioral show is immediately followed by a narrated label of the same emotion, the label must go.
- **Genre note**: Omniscient narration and fast-paced thrillers may use told emotion as efficient compression — judge by whether the telling adds information or kills the showing's effect. Don't police all telling — police UNNECESSARY telling.
- **Example**:
  > "Elle serra les poings si fort que ses ongles s'enfoncerent dans sa paume. Elle etait furieuse."
  > → Show-then-tell — la premiere phrase montre, la deuxieme tue l'effet en expliquant.
  > → Couper "Elle etait furieuse." La paume ensanglantee suffit.

### dialogue-effect

- **Detects**: Dialogue without subtext. Characters saying exactly what they mean. No gap between words and intentions. Dialogue that only exchanges information. All characters speaking with the same voice. Over-tagged dialogue ("dit-il avec colere") where the dialogue itself should carry the tone.
- **Works**: Characters say one thing, mean another. Distinct voices — recognizable without tags. Dialogue advances the scene AND reveals character. Subtext creates a second conversation underneath the words. Tags are minimal ("dit") or replaced by action beats.
- **Fails**: Characters explain their feelings in dialogue. Every line delivers information with no undertow. All characters speak in the same register and rhythm. Tags tell emotions the dialogue should carry.
- **Fix pattern**: Add what's unsaid. Give one character a hidden agenda. Differentiate voices through vocabulary, rhythm, and what each character refuses to say. Replace emotional tags with action beats.
- **Mechanism markers**: "Talking heads" — dialogue replaceable by a summary without losing emotional content. Characters naming their emotions ("je suis triste"). Dialogue that reads like Q&A. The "cover test": cover the tags — can you tell who's speaking? If all voices are interchangeable, differentiation has failed. Tags with adverbs ("dit-il tristement") = the dialogue isn't doing its job.
- **Genre note**: In romance, dialogue carries attraction through banter, avoidance, or charged silence. In horror, dialogue is the last thread of normalcy before it breaks. In thriller, dialogue carries threat and information under pressure.
- **Example**:
  > "— Je suis en colere contre toi, dit-il avec colere. — Je sais, et je suis desole, repondit-elle tristement."
  > → Dialogue frontal + tags emotionnels redondants — aucun sous-texte.
  > → "— Tu as change la serrure. — Le serrurier etait disponible." La colere passe par le sujet detourne.

### psychic-distance

- **Detects**: Uncontrolled shifts in narrative distance within a scene. Gardner's scale runs from maximum distance ("It was winter of the year 1853") to maximum closeness ("She could taste the metal in her throat"). Shifts must be deliberate. Also: sustained distance during moments that need closeness, or sustained closeness during moments that need scope.
- **Works**: Distance matches emotional need. Close for intimate, emotional, visceral moments. Distant for scope, context, irony. Transitions between levels are smooth. Common effective pattern: open at medium distance, move close at the emotional peak, pull back slightly for exit.
- **Fails**: Emotional climax told from authorial distance. Reflective passage suddenly zooming to visceral closeness. The narrator's voice intrudes where the character's interiority should dominate. Head-hopping.
- **Fix pattern**: Identify the emotional peak. Move to close narration there. Open and close at medium distance. If authorial commentary is needed, place it before or after the peak, not during.
- **Mechanism markers**: Authorial commentary interrupting close narration ("on pouvait observer que..."). Sudden shifts from interiority to omniscient observation within a paragraph. Named emotions in close-distance scenes (close narration shows, doesn't name). Style register shifts within a paragraph (intimate → essayistic → intimate). The "position test": is the reader IN the character or WATCHING the character? Unless the shift is deliberate for irony, it's a break.
- **Example**:
  > "Son coeur se brisa. On pouvait observer que, dans ce genre de situations, les etres humains reagissent souvent de maniere imprevisible."
  > → Rupture de distance — narration intime suivie d'un commentaire d'auteur distant.
  > → Rester en narration proche : "Son coeur se brisa. Elle ne savait plus ou mettre ses mains."
