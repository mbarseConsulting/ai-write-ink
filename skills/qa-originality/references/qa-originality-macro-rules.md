# Macro Rules — qa-originality

> Scope: macro
> Loaded when: text >= multiple chapters or full manuscript
> Evaluates: Manuscript-level originality — concept, arc, ambition, identity
> Preferred output: assessment

Verdict-dominated: originality at this scale is a global assessment.

---

## Verdict Axes

### concept-originality

- **Assesses**: Premise, hook, central idea. Has THIS specific combination been done?
- **Works**: Not "is it a fantasy novel" but "is it a fantasy novel where [specific combination] produces something genuinely new?" Originality is often in the combination, not the ingredients.
- **Fails**: The premise is a known formula. The hook has been done. The central idea feels pre-owned. Nothing in the concept makes a reader say "I haven't read THIS before."
- **States**: genuinely new / fresh combination / familiar with new texture / derivative
- **Suggestion pattern**: If familiar with new texture → amplify the texture that makes it different — push the unique element harder. If derivative → identify which ingredient is borrowed and replace it, or combine ingredients no one has combined before.

### arc-originality

- **Assesses**: Story structure, ending, character trajectories. Predictable from page 1, or invisible logic with surprise?
- **Works**: An arc that feels both surprising and the only possible path. The reader couldn't have predicted it, but looking back, it was inevitable.
- **Fails**: A plot that follows expected genre beats. Character arc hitting every predicted milestone. The reader always knows what's coming.
- **States**: surprising-inevitable / some surprises / predictable / formulaic
- **Suggestion pattern**: If predictable → identify the moments where the expected thing happens and swerve at ONE of them — the rest can follow convention if one beat breaks it. If formulaic → map the formula explicitly and violate it at the structural level.

### ambition

- **Assesses**: Scope, difficulty, formal challenge. Is the author attempting something hard?
- **Works**: The author is attempting something new for the genre, something that could fail spectacularly. The difficulty is visible. The ambition earns the reader's respect even if execution is imperfect.
- **Fails**: Safe choices throughout. No risk-taking. The book does exactly what similar books do, competently. It will be forgotten.
- **States**: ambitious / competent / safe / timid
- **Suggestion pattern**: If safe → what would this book look like if the author wasn't afraid of failing? Identify the safe choice and propose the dangerous one. If timid → the author needs permission to take risks — identify where restraint is hurting the work.

### artistic-identity

- **Assesses**: Overall impression, memorability, unity of vision. Is this book irreplaceable?
- **Works**: You could describe what makes THIS book THIS book and not any other. It has a unique fingerprint. It's irreplaceable in the reader's library.
- **Fails**: The book is interchangeable with its shelf neighbors. Competent but without identity. Nothing makes it THIS book specifically.
- **States**: irreplaceable / memorable / competent / interchangeable
- **Suggestion pattern**: If competent → identify the one element that feels most uniquely the author's and build the book's identity around it. If interchangeable → what does this author know, feel, or see that no one else does? That's the identity — it needs to permeate the work.

### maturity

- **Assesses**: Confidence, control, intentionality. Does the author know what they're doing?
- **Works**: Deliberate choices including deliberate imperfections. The author's hand is steady, sure. Effects are controlled. The reader feels authorial confidence.
- **Fails**: Instinct without craft. The author follows impulse without understanding the effect. Or: over-control that kills spontaneity. Both signal immaturity.
- **States**: masterful / confident / developing / uncontrolled
- **Suggestion pattern**: If developing → identify the strongest controlled passage and use it as evidence of what the author CAN do — apply that control more broadly. If uncontrolled → the author needs to read their own work as a reader — what effect did they intend, and what effect did they create?
